Book: Wayward Saints, by Ronald W. Walker (1998)20080121
<p>This book tells the story of the Godbeite rebellion in *REDACTED*ism in the late 1800s. It is very interesting to see the ideas of the group of people responsible for it and where they are today. For example, the authority of Church leaders remains un-changed in form, but not in scope, as the political and economic kingdoms have passed away. I think that Buddhism is the new Spiritualism for modern Godbeites to partake in for additional spiritual experiences that modern *REDACTED*ism does not give. All the other ideas of Godbeites now find their home, in my opinion, in the Sunstone community. Given this and the history of the Godbeite movement, it is not hard to understand why the Church reacted as it did in the early nineties towards Sunstone et al.: Church authorities, whether conscience of it or not, are likely influenced by the official reaction to Godbeites in the past.</p>

<p>Another thought on this vein: It seems understandable why many members of the Church (and perhaps leaders, as well) would be uncomfortable with Joseph Smith's use of a seer stone in the translation of the Book of *REDACTED* given the prominence of spiritualism in the Godbeite movement and the harshness of Church leaders in their sermons against such spiritualism.</p>

<p>The writing and research of the book is very good, but on occasion there is an inkling that Walker has an agenda. There are many times when he uses psychological speculation to explain an actor's motives, rather than simply relating what happened, when there is no historical record of their own explanation. Similarly, he brings up information in strange places, for example, there is frequent mention and dwelling on the Spiritualist manifestations of the Godbeite crowd, even when all that can be said is that <em>they</em> didn't mention it. Overall, Walker seems to assume (like many Latter-day Saints) that the only result of disobedience and failure of testimony is worldliness and sin, and he seems to go out of his way to paint the characters in this light whenever possible.</p>
