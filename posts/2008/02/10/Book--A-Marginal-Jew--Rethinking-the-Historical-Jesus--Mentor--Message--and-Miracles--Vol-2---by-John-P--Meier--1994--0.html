Book: A Marginal Jew: Rethinking the Historical Jesus: Mentor, Message, and Miracles, Vol 2., by John P. Meier (1994)20080210
<p>This second volume studies John the Baptist, on his own and in his relation to Jesus, as well as the "Kingdom of God" message of Jesus' ministry, and finally the purported miracles of Jesus.</p>

<p>The methodology of the study of the miracles is interesting. His only goal is to verify that Jesus claimed or was believed to have performed miracles in his own time, independent of whether these can <em>really</em> be called "miracles".</p>
<blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>To be blunt, without miracles, one wonders how much popularity this particular Jewish preacher and teacher would have enjoyed. Without miracles, many Palestinian Jews might have seen Jesus merely as a more "upbeat" version of John the Baptist, an echo of his former master but lacking the master's striking asceticism and stark venue. Without miracles, the figure, fame, and fate of Jesus would have been quite different and probably quite diminished. Without miracles, one is left with the paper cutout manufactured by Thomas Jefferson and all subsequent questers for a reasonable Jesus. [p. 4]</p>
</blockquote>
</blockquote>

<p>He points out that "miracle" is not a historical category, but a theological one. He also spends a good deal of time contrasting the stories of Jesus' miracles with typical stories of "magic" and attempts to show that they are different beasts.</p>

<p>Interesting mention: "In the Genesis Apocryphon, Abraham is besought by Pharaoh to pray and lay hands on him so that the plague and the evil spirit that afflict Pharaoh and his people may depart (1QapGen 20:16-29)" (p. 405)</p>

<p>"This really works" in a Greek spell-book. Hah. (p. 546)</p>

<p>One thing I noticed a lot in this book is the assumption that the authors of the Gospels choose every single word, narrative pattern, etc. explicitly and with a great deal of thought. Now, it is certainly the case that they cared a lot about it and tried to create patterns. But, it is also a certainty that at least some of the patterns and exegesis of the last two thousand years were not originally intended. This point seems to be missed by many.</p>
