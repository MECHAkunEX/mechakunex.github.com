Book: No god, but God: Egypt and the Triumph of Islam, by Geneive Abdo (2000)20060314
An interesting book about the growth of Islam in Egypt, which was
once a highly Westernized country in the Middle East.</p>

<p>I confused this book with <a
href='http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/1400062136'><cite>No
god, but God</cite></a>, by Reza Aslan, but I enjoyed this book
very much.</p>

<p>Although it may be fulfillment of the sentiment, I always find this interesting:</p>

<blockquote>
<p>During my periodic travels to the West, I was truck how editors, publishers, literary agents, and friends wanted to discuss virtually nothing but my impressions of the status of women under Islam. Most of their questions were based on the assumption that Islamic practices were forced on women, too helpless and too powerless in male-dominated societies to control their own destinies. No topic aroused as much interest and visceral emotion as veiling. When I explained that veiling was practice voluntarily by an increasing number of Muslim women, Westerners often offered a counter-explanation--if veiling was in fact by choice, then women certainly were being brainwashed by Muslim extremists. Such views are little removed from the prevailing attitudes of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries among colonial officials, missionaries, and Western feminists in the Arab and Islamic world. The veil was seen as a prime symbol of all that was backward, underdeveloped--in short, non-Western. The attack on the covering or seclusion of Muslim women, often justified in the name of modernization and the cultural superiority of European ways, supported a broader assault on native society in general. The <em>hijab</em> was fair game for Western critics of all stripes and persuasions and it remains so to this day. [p. 141]</p>
</blockquote>
