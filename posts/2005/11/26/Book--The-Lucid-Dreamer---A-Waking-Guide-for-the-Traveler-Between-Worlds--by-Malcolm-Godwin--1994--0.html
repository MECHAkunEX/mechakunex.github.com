Book: The Lucid Dreamer - A Waking Guide for the Traveler Between Worlds, by Malcolm Godwin (1994)20051126
<p>The problem with this book is that the author could have written a twenty-five page pamplet filled up with fluff, hyperbole and wild claims. But, instead he wrote a two-hundred fifty page book filled up with pretty pictures and the same text over and over again. One gets the impression that instead of actually making a convincing case, Godwin thought that by repeating his claims, they would be believed. This may be a larger problem associated with "coffee table" books that do not anticipate being read through in one sitting.</p>

<p>Godwin is a typical guy obsessed with Eastern culture and has no problem bashing what he thinks the West is and praising what he thinks the East is.</p>

<p>However, the no-nonsense advice of this book is:</p>
<ul>
<li>Practice mental visualization of objects, for example, look at something then recreate it mentally and experiment with rotation and manipulation. This will exercise your visualization skills and may be useful in attaining lucidity.</li>
<li>Attempt to remember dreams and their pattersn to increase familiarity with them. This will make recognition of dreaming while dreaming more likely. The best way to do this is with a dream journal, kept by the bed and used constantly.</li>
<li>Before sleeping, retrace the entire day in reverse to exercise memory and clear your head. This will also help you anticipate what may appear in your dreams.</li>
<li>Before sleeping, think of your current problems and commit to finding solutions to them in sleep, the previous item will help with this.</li>
<li>Memorize dreams and intend to wake up in them. This conscious intention is similar to intending to wake up at a certain hour in the morning without an alarm.</li>
<li>Visualize something---your hands, for example---during the day. Before sleeping, think about how when you see them next, you will be dreaming and use this to achieve lucidity.</li>
<li>Consider the reality of things in dreams at more advanced levels. Apparently, lucid dreaming can be very confusing.</li>
</ul>
