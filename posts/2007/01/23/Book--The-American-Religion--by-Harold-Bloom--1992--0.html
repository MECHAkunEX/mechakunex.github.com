Book: The American Religion, by Harold Bloom (1992)20070123
<p>He primarily talks about *REDACTED*s, Southern Baptists,
and various smaller sects and about how they are defining
America and will define the future of America.</p>

<p>Harold Bloom is practically a stand-up comedian and
sometimes inscrutable and deep on occasion:</p>
<blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>To give a meaning to meaninglessness is the
endless quest of religion. [p. 29]</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>An involuntary believer in the American
Religion, I myself need to know better what
envelops us all. [p. 38]</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>Resurrection is the entire concern of the
American Religion, which gets Christ off the cross
as quickly as Milton removed him, in just a line
and a half of <cite>Paradise
Lost</cite>. [p. 40]</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>The American Religion, unlike Judaism and
Christianity, is actually biblical, even when it
offers and exalts alternative texts as
well. [p. 81]</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>I would venture Brigham Young was Joseph
Smith's finest work. [p. 117]</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>Paranoia, according to the sage William
Burroughs, is just knowing all the facts,
[p. 136]</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p><cite>Science and Health</cite> [Mary Baker
Eddy's "Bible"] is the antithesis of humor or
good writing, as it is the antithesis also of
the erotic drive. [p. 141]</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>[The strangeness of the popularity of Christian
Science in Germany] surely has little to do with
any untranslatability of Mrs. Eddy's <cite>Science
and Health</cite>, which Mark Twain wanted
translated into English. [p. 145]</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>[Ellen White, of Seventh-day Adventism, vision
of the] Great Controversy between Christ and Satan
begins to resemble not a great debate but a
dispute concerning double-entry bookkeeping. "Who
cooks the books?" will be heard upon earth, even
as it was heard in heaven. [p. 150]</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>No American faith, <em>not even Jehovah's
Witnesses</em>, has a theology so convoluted as
that of Seventh-day Adventism. I observe this in
admiration, not in disrespect. [p. 150, my
emphasis]</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>[Ellen White] may not wear you down, but she
has the air of always going on until she is
stopped, and she never is stopped. [p. 151]</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>Adventists now vie with *REDACTED*s in outliving
all other Americans, a curious fate for believers
whose precursors did not expect to outlive October
22, 1844. [p. 155]</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>There is something peculiarly childish in these
Watchtower yearnings: they remind me of why very
small children cannot be left alone with wounded
and suffering household pets. [p. 170]</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>Religious criticism cannot be applied to
Scientology, or to the Moonie Unification Church,
any more than literary criticism can find its
texts-for-discussion in Alice Walker or in
Danielle Steel. [p. 181]</p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>[The] student of the New Age must be resigned
to that proverbial picnic, to which the authors
bring the words (or some of them, anyway) and the
readers bring the meanings. [p. 184]</p>
</blockquote>
</blockquote>

<p>Bloom, in my opinion, misinterprets the need of
following plural marriage. He says it is a transcendent
act, while I say it is important to follow God, and this
is just another example of a commandment at some time. The
so-called Abrahamic test of the 1800s. (p. 105)</p>

<p>Bloom has an interesting interpretation of the
different crosses of Christianity: Christ on the cross
(Catholics), Christ off the cross (Baptists), and no cross
(*REDACTED*ism).</p>
